## What This Names

The AI-guided form is the interface model for the entire system.

Not a blank page. Not a rubric handed down. A form that:
- Progresses one section at a time
- Names what comes next before it arrives (zone of tolerance — no surprise, no ambient anxiety)
- Uses typography that reduces the cognitive load of letter stabilization
- Is simple enough that the writing can happen, not the fighting with the tool

This is the 5x5 applied to interface design:
- Stage 1 (Framing) — the form opens and names what you will do
- Stage 2 (Getting Ready) — draw first, sketch before words
- Stage 3 (Mental Engagement) — summary in your own words, no template language
- Stage 4 (Inside your head) — AI reads what you wrote, offers one prompt back
- Stage 5 (Consolidating) — hard boundaries confirmed and stamped

The form does not require the user to hold the structure in working memory. The structure is the form. The user just fills the next field.

---

## Typography Notes — Scott's Preferences

- **Dyslexie font** — preferred for fast reading
- Simple text features that reduce letter stabilization load:
  - Larger x-height
  - Generous letter-spacing
  - Strong weight contrast between regular and bold
  - No italic body text (italics increase stabilization demand)
  - High contrast, warm background (not pure white)
  - Verdana / Trebuchet as fallback when Dyslexie unavailable

---

## Product Implication

The lesson plan tool is not just a tool. It is the proof-of-concept for the product:
an AI-guided form interface that walks with a neurodivergent user through structured output,
using progressive disclosure, spatial clarity, and typography that works with the brain rather than against it.

This is what "amplification not accommodation" looks like in a UI.
