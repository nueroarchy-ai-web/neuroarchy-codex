## PART 1 — THE PROBLEM OF PRACTICE

Two interlocking problems observed in HO1-4C:

**Problem 1 — Zone of Tolerance**
Students cannot access academic content when their nervous system is dysregulated. Regulation precedes cognition — not as a belief, but as physiological fact. High-stakes testing (CAASPP) amplifies dysregulation precisely when academic access matters most.

**Problem 2 — Tools for Focus Under Test Stress**
Neurodivergent learners require explicit structural tools — not reminders to focus, but physical and cognitive scaffolds that hold attention when ambient stress would otherwise collapse the working memory field.

**These are not separate problems.** They are the same problem at two scales: the nervous system scale and the cognitive tool scale. The intervention addresses both simultaneously.

## PART 2 — THE THEORETICAL MODEL

### The Five-Stage Learning Sequence
*Emerged through classroom observation. Original contribution. Not in the existing literature in this form.*

1. **Zone of tolerance** — physiological access. Nervous system regulated, social anxiety calm. Precondition for everything that follows.
2. **Social trust = container trust** — relational access. The relationship is safe. The teacher sees me. I am not invisible under stress. (Sofia's diagnostic and Scott's felt sense are the same condition from different angles.)
3. **Object trust** — spatial access. Things are findable. I know why I have them. If my stuff is missing or a mess, it is its own dysregulation.
4. **Semantic anchor** — cognitive access. Why this fact matters, before trying to hold it. Without the why, the detail has no place to attach.
5. **Recall** — retrieval as function, not goal. Follows from the four conditions above. Cannot be taught toward directly without the prior layers.

*Any one layer offline = learning stops. This is the sequence. It does not skip.*

### The AID Assessment Loop
*The operational method. Runs across all classroom structures.*

| Element | Function | Working Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Abductive** | Read artifact before forcing category | "What does this suggest about how this learner is thinking?" |
| **Inductive** | Track recurrence across time | "What pattern is emerging?" |
| **Deductive** | Design the next fair demonstration | "What is the fairest way to let the learning be shown?" |

**The structural claim:** The AID loop mirrors the architecture of the team that produced it. The method and the apparatus share the same cognitive logic. This is not coincidence — it is the same mind designing both.

### The Missing Third Assessment Type
Current assessment discourse operates on two engines:
- **Formative** (inductive) — reading forward from observation to adjust instruction
- **Summative** (deductive) — testing backward from standard to measure attainment

Both assume stable access to the learning state being measured. Neither works for a dysregulated nervous system.

**Abductive assessment** is the third engine: reading backward from artifacts, behavior, and pattern to infer the learning that produced them. This is the methodology of Pedagogical Documentation (Malaguzzi/Rinaldi, Reggio Emilia) and Learning Stories (Carr & Lee, Te Whāriki, NZ). 40-year international pedigree. Not previously named in California elementary assessment practice.

## PART 3 — THE INTERVENTION: THE 5x5

The 5x5 is the classroom instructional scaffold. Each stage does double work — it carries a CEL literacy standard AND addresses a zone of tolerance function simultaneously.

| Stage | Saphier Function | Zone of Tolerance Function | CEL Standards |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Framing the Learning | Objective + big picture before instruction | Reduces ambient anxiety — student knows what is coming | CEL.4.RI.2, CEL.4.W.2 |
| 2. Getting Ready | Activate prior knowledge | Connects new to known — container trust through familiarity | CEL.4.RI.1, CEL.4.S.1 |
| 3. Creating Mental Engagement | Explicit directions, no steps left to chance | Removes uncertainty trigger — object trust activated | CEL.4.RI.1, CEL.4.W.2 |
| 4. Getting Inside Students' Heads | Dipsticking, reading body language | Social trust — teacher sees me, I am not invisible | CEL.4.S.1, CEL.4.RI.2 |
| 5. Consolidating and Anchoring | Summarizing — pulling it together at close | Semantic anchor set — now I know why this mattered | CEL.4.W.2, CEL.4.W.3 |

**The 5x5 is not a lesson plan template.** It is a zone of tolerance scaffold embedded in a literacy structure. The structure IS the intervention.

## PART 4 — THE METHODOLOGY

**Pedagogical documentation** — teacher as researcher, reading backward from artifacts to infer learning. Malaguzzi/Rinaldi (Reggio Emilia), Carr & Lee (Learning Stories, NZ).

**Abductive method** — Wong and Kodagoda (2016): anchoring, laddering, associations.
- *Anchoring* — low-interpretation coding of salient data (the drawing, the posture, the half-finished note)
- *Laddering* — deliberate movement up and down abstraction levels to see pattern
- *Associations* — lateral linking of codes to generate alternative explanations

**Triangulation — three source types:**
1. Artifacts — student work, drawings, structured notes, board photos (May 1, 2026)
2. Observations — regulatory state, body language, engagement during each 5x5 stage; tallies and surveys
3. Reflections — teacher-researcher voice across the instructional arc

**CAASPP** — one data point confirming the pattern the documentation already showed. Not the sole measure. The argument: the reflective practice is *how* they got to CAASPP performance. Not despite it — through it.

**Teacher-researcher posture:** Scott's own neurodivergent cognitive architecture (Eide & Eide, 2011 — Interconnected, Dynamic, Narrative reasoning) is precisely what makes the abductive reading possible. The teacher-researcher and the teaching method are the same instrument. This is not a limitation to be disclosed — it is a cited theoretical asset.
