## One-Paragraph Model Description (paper-ready)

This study employs an abductive, neurodiversity- and trauma-informed assessment model that treats classroom and workplace data as clues in an open investigation rather than as evidence for pre-selected deficit categories. Drawing on Wong and Kodagoda's (2016) framework of anchoring, laddering, and associations, observational episodes, narratives, and performance artifacts are first coded at a low level of interpretation (anchors), then reorganized across levels of abstraction (ladders), and finally linked laterally (associations) to generate and refine multiple plausible explanations for learner behavior. The model leverages dyslexic strengths in interconnected, narrative, and dynamic reasoning (Eide & Eide, 2011; Rappolt-Schlichtmann, Boucher, & Evans, 2018; West, 2017) by privileging visual, spatial, and story-based representations of emerging patterns over purely linear summaries. At the same time, it aligns with trauma-informed and resilience-oriented frameworks that emphasize safety, predictability, and collaboration by treating affective signals and apparent "noncompliance" as data to be interpreted within context and by prioritizing adjustments to environment, routines, and instructional clarity before attributing difficulties to individual pathology.

## The Three Operations (Wong & Kodagoda, 2016)

- **Anchoring** — initial, low-interpretation coding of salient data (the drawing, the posture, the half-finished note)
- **Laddering** — deliberate movement up and down levels of abstraction to see patterns
- **Associations** — lateral linking of codes and themes to generate alternative explanations

These are the three moves Scott makes in the classroom daily. Now they have a name and a citation.
